EGARCH Model: Asymmetric Volatility Forecast

Master EGARCH asymmetric volatility modeling for financial risk. Capture leverage effects where volatility increases more after negative returns than positive.

Quick Answer

EGARCH captures leverage effect: negative returns increase volatility more than positive. Log(variance) = ω + α·z + γ(|z|-E|z|) + β·log(prev variance). Essential for equity markets.

EGARCH Formula

EGARCH(1,1) model:

ε_t = σ_t · z_t
log(σ²_t) = ω + α·z_{t-1} + γ(|z_{t-1}| - E|z|) + β·log(σ²_{t-1})

Where:
σ²_t = Conditional variance at time t
z_t = Standardized residual (ε_t/σ_t)
E|z| = Expected absolute value of z
α = Symmetric shock impact
γ = Asymmetric leverage coefficient (γ<0 = leverage)
β = Volatility persistence

Leverage effect: γ < 0 → Negative returns increase volatility more

How EGARCH Captures Leverage

EGARCH models volatility in logs, ensuring positivity without constraints. The asymmetry term γ·(|z_{t-1}| - E|z|) distinguishes between positive and negative shocks. When γ<0, negative z (bad returns) produce higher volatility than positive z (good returns) of the same magnitude. This captures the leverage effect: equity volatility rises more during market declines.

Financial Applications

Equity Volatility

EGARCH is preferred for stocks due to leverage effect. Capture asymmetric volatility response to positive and negative returns. Critical for market crash analysis.

Stress Testing

EGARCH forecasts volatility during market stress more accurately than GARCH. Better for scenario analysis and stress testing frameworks.

Derivative Pricing

Asymmetric volatility improves option pricing for equities. Better captures volatility smile and skew patterns.

Risk Management

EGARCH provides more accurate VaR for equity portfolios during downturns. Critical for left-tail risk measurement.

Parameter Interpretation

Leverage Parameter (γ)

γ<0 indicates leverage effect. Magnitude shows asymmetry strength. For S&P 500, γ typically -0.1 to -0.2. Test significance with t-statistic.

Persistence

Persistence measured by β. Near 1 indicates slow mean reversion. Unlike GARCH, persistence not simply α+β due to log transformation.

News Impact Curve

Plot volatility vs return shock. Shows asymmetric response. Essential for understanding model behavior under different market scenarios.

Nelson's Innovation

Daniel Nelson (1991) proposed EGARCH to address GARCH limitations. The exponential specification solved positivity constraints and allowed asymmetric volatility response. EGARCH became the standard for modeling equity volatility, particularly during turbulent periods.

Implementation Tips

Use rugarch in R or arch in Python. Start with GARCH(1,1) then test EGARCH. Check γ significance with Wald test. Consider Student-t distribution for robust estimation.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the leverage effect in finance?

Leverage effect refers to volatility increasing more after negative returns than positive returns. It's driven by financial leverage: falling equity values increase debt-to-equity ratios, amplifying volatility.

When to choose EGARCH over GARCH?

Choose EGARCH for equity returns where leverage effect is documented. For currencies or commodities, standard GARCH may suffice. Test γ significance to decide.

Can EGARCH have negative persistence?

No, β is typically positive (0.8-0.98). Negative β would indicate mean reversion which is unusual for volatility. The log specification allows negative coefficients but β should be positive.
