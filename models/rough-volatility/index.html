Rough Heston Model:

Volatility Dynamics:
σ_t = σ₀·exp(∫₀ᵗ f(s-t)dW_s)

Where f(s-t) ∝ (s-t)^{H-1/2} (power kernel)

Or fractional Brownian motion:
B_t^H = (1/Γ(H+1/2))∫₀ᵗ (t-s)^{H-1/2}dW_s

With Hurst parameter H < 0.5

Asset dynamics:
dS_t = rS_t dt + σ_t S_t dW_t

Variance process:
dv_t = κ(θ - v_t)dt + ξ·v_t^γ dW_t^H

Where W_t^H is fractional Brownian motion

How Rough Volatility Works

Rough volatility models represent a paradigm shift in volatility modeling. Instead of smooth volatility processes, they use fractional Brownian motion with Hurst parameter H < 0.5. This creates the 'rough' behavior observed in realized volatility data. The model has long memory and captures the persistence of volatility shocks. The rough Heston model is a prominent example, replacing Brownian motion with fractional Brownian motion in the variance process.

The Rough Volatility Revolution

Gatheral, Jaisson, and Rosenbaum (2018) provided empirical evidence that volatility is rough with H < 0.5. This challenged the assumption of smooth volatility processes in models like Heston. Rough volatility has become a major research area in quantitative finance.

Implementation Tips

Use fast fractional Brownian motion generators (circulant embedding). Implement rough Heston carefully with fractional integration. Use variance reduction techniques for Monte Carlo.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does 'rough' volatility mean?

Rough volatility means the volatility process has Hurst parameter H < 0.5, making it rougher than Brownian motion. This is observed in realized volatility data.

What is the Hurst parameter in rough volatility?

H measures the roughness of the volatility process. H < 0.5 indicates anti-persistence (roughness). Empirical estimates often find H ≈ 0.1-0.2.

Can rough volatility be used for trading?

Yes, rough volatility improves volatility forecasts and option pricing. However, implementation requires sophisticated techniques and is computationally intensive.
