Fractional Kelly: Balanced Position Sizing for Risk Management

Master Fractional Kelly position sizing for optimal risk-reward balance. Learn how half-Kelly and quarter-Kelly reduce volatility while preserving growth rate.

Quick Answer

Fractional Kelly (25-50% of full) reduces volatility while preserving most growth. Half-Kelly cuts drawdown risk from 50% to 25% while achieving 75% of growth.

Fractional Kelly Formula

Full Kelly:
f* = (b*p - q) / b

Fractional Kelly:
f_fractional = k × f*

Where:
k = fraction of full Kelly (0 to 1)

Growth Rate:
G(k) = p*ln(1 + k*f**b) + q*ln(1 - k*f*)

Volatility Reduction:
σ(k) = k × σ(full)

Drawdown Reduction:
DD(k) = k × DD(full)

Why Fractional Kelly Works

Fractional Kelly reduces position size by a constant factor. This linear scaling reduces both volatility and return proportionally. However, due to the convexity of the growth function, growth rate decreases less than linearly. A 50% reduction in size reduces volatility by 75% but growth by only 25%. This favorable trade-off makes fractional Kelly optimal for risk-averse investors. The Sharpe ratio typically improves as the fraction decreases, as volatility falls faster than return.

Calculating Fractional Kelly Position Size

1

Calculate full Kelly fraction f* from strategy metrics

2

Choose fraction k based on risk tolerance (0.25-0.5 typical)

3

Compute fractional Kelly: f = k × f*

4

Multiply by account balance for dollar risk

5

Divide by stop loss distance for position size

6

Cap at maximum position size (e.g., 25% of capital)

7

Recalculate as edge changes over time

Fractional Kelly Applications

Multi-Strategy Portfolio

Use quarter-Kelly per strategy. Allows 4-8 uncorrelated strategies to run simultaneously with manageable total risk.

Conservative Trading

Professional traders use half-Kelly for main strategy, quarter-Kelly for exploratory strategies. Reduces blowup risk significantly.

Institutional Asset Allocation

Pension funds and endowments use 10-25% Kelly to ensure capital preservation while capturing some growth benefits.

System Development

Use fractional Kelly during system testing. Quarter-Kelly allows real trading while limiting drawdown during system refinement.

Fraction Selection Guide

Fraction = 0.5 (Half-Kelly)

Professional standard. Best for active traders with proven edge. 75% less volatility, 75% of growth.

Fraction = 0.25 (Quarter-Kelly)

Institutional standard. Best for multi-strategy portfolios. 90% less volatility, 50% of growth.

Fraction = 0.1 (Tenth-Kelly)

Conservative allocation. Best for large funds diversifying across 10+ strategies. Minimal risk.

Fraction = 0.33 (One-Third Kelly)

Intermediate option. Balance between growth and risk. Popular among quantitative hedge funds.

Development of Fractional Kelly

Practioners realized full Kelly created excessive volatility. Edward Thorp used half-Kelly in his hedge fund. Bill Ziemba and other researchers documented the favorable risk-reward trade-off of fractional betting. Today, fractional Kelly is standard in quantitative finance, with most professional firms using 25-50% of full Kelly.

Growth Rate Comparison

For full Kelly: G* = p*ln(1+f*b) + q*ln(1-f)

For fractional Kelly (k fraction): G(k) = p*ln(1+k*f*b) + q*ln(1-k*f)

Taylor expansion for small f:
G(k) ≈ G* * k - (1/2)*f² * k² * (p*b² + q)

Growth reduction: ΔG ≈ (1 - k) * G* + O(f²)
Volatility reduction: σ(k) ≈ k * σ*

Common Mistakes

Using too high fraction for risk tolerance (overbetting). Not reducing fraction for correlated strategies. Ignoring fat tails in return distribution. Failing to account for estimation error in win probability. Using fixed fraction without recalculating as edge changes.

Frequently Asked Questions

What fraction should I use for my trading?

Start with quarter-Kelly (25%) for new strategies. If proven over 200+ trades with consistent edge, consider half-Kelly (50%). Full Kelly only for very high conviction.

How does fractional Kelly affect diversification?

With quarter-Kelly per strategy, you can run 4 uncorrelated strategies with same total risk as one full-Kelly strategy. This improves Sharpe ratio significantly.

Can I change fractions dynamically?

Yes, many traders use higher fractions during high-conviction periods and lower fractions during uncertain times. Be aware of increased transaction costs from adjusting.
