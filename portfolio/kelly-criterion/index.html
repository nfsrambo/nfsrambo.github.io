Kelly Formula

Full Kelly Criterion:
f* = (bp - q) / b

Where:
f* = fraction of capital to bet
b = net odds received (profit on win / loss on loss)
p = probability of winning
q = probability of losing = 1-p

For binary outcomes with win amount W and loss amount L:
f* = (p*W - q*L) / (W*L)

Geometric Growth Rate:
G(f) = p * ln(1 + f*b) + q * ln(1 - f)

Optimal f maximizes G(f) where derivative = 0

How the Kelly Criterion Works

The Kelly Criterion maximizes the expected geometric growth rate of your capital. Unlike arithmetic mean, geometric mean accounts for compounding effects and the fact that losses reduce the base for future gains. The formula balances the trade-off between risk and reward, suggesting larger bets when the edge is higher and smaller bets when uncertainty increases. For trading, Kelly size corresponds to the percentage of capital to risk on each trade.

Applying Kelly to Trading

1

Calculate historical win rate from backtest (p)

2

Determine average win amount and loss amount

3

Compute net odds: b = (avg_win / avg_loss)

4

Apply formula: f* = (b*p - (1-p)) / b

5

Adjust for overbetting: use 25-50% of f*

6

Calculate position size: Capital × f* × (1 / stop_loss_percent)

7

Monitor and recalculate as win rate changes

Practical Applications

Strategy Allocation

Allocate capital to multiple strategies based on their Kelly fractions. Strategies with higher Kelly get larger allocations, maximizing overall portfolio growth.

Risk Management

Use Kelly as maximum risk limit. Never bet more than full Kelly. Half-Kelly provides 75% volatility reduction while maintaining most growth.

System Comparison

Compare trading systems by their Kelly fractions. Higher Kelly indicates stronger edge and more potential for compounding.

Dynamic Sizing

Adjust position size as edge changes. Recalculate Kelly after each trade or periodically to adapt to market conditions.

Pros and Cons

Advantages

  • -Maximizes long-term wealth growth mathematically
  • -Accounts for compounding effects
  • -Prevents overbetting and ruin
  • -Easy to calculate with basic edge estimates
  • -Provides clear position sizing rule

Limitations

  • -Requires accurate win probability estimates
  • -Full Kelly leads to 50% drawdown risk
  • -Assumes independent identical bets
  • -Doesn't account for market correlations
  • -Can suggest aggressive sizing with estimation errors

Practical Implementation Tips

Estimate Cautiously

Reduce win probability estimates by 10-20% to account for overfitting. Kelly is sensitive to overestimation of edge.

Use Fractional Kelly

Start with half-Kelly (50% of full) for most strategies. Quarter-Kelly for conservative approaches.

Set Maximum Size

Cap position size at 15-25% regardless of Kelly calculation. Diversification constraints override pure Kelly.

Recalculate Periodically

Update win probability estimates every 50-100 trades. Edge changes over time as markets evolve.

Historical Development

John L. Kelly Jr., a researcher at Bell Labs, published the criterion in 1956 based on information theory. Edward Thorp applied Kelly to blackjack and later to investing, achieving exceptional returns. Warren Buffett and other value investors use a variant (focusing on high-probability, high-return opportunities). The criterion remains controversial but mathematically proven for maximizing geometric growth.

Derivation

Maximize G(f) = p*ln(1+f*b) + q*ln(1-f)

dG/df = (p*b)/(1+f*b) - q/(1-f) = 0

(p*b)/(1+f*b) = q/(1-f)

p*b*(1-f) = q*(1+f*b)

p*b - p*b*f = q + q*b*f

p*b - q = f*(p*b² + q*b)

f = (p*b - q) / (b*(p*b + q))

Since p*b + q = p*b + (1-p) = 1 + p(b-1)

For b=1 (even money): f = p - q = 2p - 1

Common Mistakes

Overestimating win probability (backtest bias). Using full Kelly without drawdown tolerance. Ignoring correlation between bets. Not recalculating as edge changes. Betting Kelly on non-repeatable opportunities. Failing to account for transaction costs and slippage.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the Kelly Criterion suitable for all trading styles?

Best for strategies with quantifiable edge and many repeated trades (100+). Less suitable for long-term investing or strategies with few trades where estimates are unreliable.

What happens if I bet more than Kelly?

Betting above Kelly reduces growth rate and increases risk. Betting double Kelly often leads to negative expected growth despite positive edge.

How do I handle multiple concurrent positions?

Use fractional allocation: reduce Kelly fraction by number of uncorrelated positions. For 4 uncorrelated trades, use 1/4 of Kelly per position for equivalent total risk.
