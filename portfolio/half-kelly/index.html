Half-Kelly: The Professional Standard for Position Sizing

Master Half-Kelly position sizing, the most widely used variant among professional traders. Learn why 50% of full Kelly balances growth and risk for optimal long-term results.

Quick Answer

Half-Kelly uses 50% of full Kelly. Reduces volatility 75% while preserving 75% of growth. Professional standard with 25% maximum drawdown.

Half-Kelly Formula

Full Kelly:
f* = (b*p - q) / b

Half-Kelly:
f_half = 0.5 × f*

Growth Rate Comparison:
G_full = p*ln(1+f*b) + q*ln(1-f)
G_half = p*ln(1+0.5f*b) + q*ln(1-0.5f)

For typical parameters (p=0.55, b=2):
f* = 0.175 (17.5%)
f_half = 0.0875 (8.75%)

Drawdown Reduction:
DD_half ≈ 0.5 × DD_full (about 25% vs 50%)

Why Half-Kelly is Optimal

Half-Kelly represents the sweet spot in the risk-reward trade-off. The growth function's convexity means reducing position size by half reduces volatility by three-quarters but only reduces growth by one-quarter. This super-linear risk reduction makes half-Kelly superior to both full Kelly and fractional Kelly below 50% for most traders. The Sharpe ratio typically peaks near 50% of full Kelly, as volatility reduction outweighs return reduction.

Half-Kelly Applications

Quantitative Hedge Funds

Industry standard for directional strategies. Half-Kelly balances growth with institutional drawdown constraints (typical 15-25% limit).

Proprietary Trading

Prop traders often use half-Kelly as maximum risk per strategy. Allows 2-3 concurrent strategies within 50% total risk budget.

Systematic Trend Following

Trend followers use half-Kelly to survive long drawdown periods while capturing large trends. Reduces risk of ruin significantly.

Options Selling Strategies

High win rate strategies (70-80%) benefit from half-Kelly. Reduces tail risk while maintaining attractive returns.

Practical Half-Kelly Rules

Maximum Position Size

Cap at 15-20% of capital regardless of Kelly calculation. Diversification limits override pure Kelly.

Strategy Allocation

For multiple strategies, allocate half-Kelly per strategy. Total allocation may exceed 100% due to diversification benefits.

Drawdown Management

Reduce to quarter-Kelly after 15-20% drawdown. Return to half-Kelly after recovery. Protects during difficult periods.

Edge Validation

Require 100+ trades with positive expectancy before using half-Kelly. Use quarter-Kelly during validation.

Adoption of Half-Kelly

Edward Thorp popularized half-Kelly in his hedge fund after experiencing full Kelly volatility. Subsequent research by Ziemba, MacLean, and others demonstrated half-Kelly's optimal properties. Today, half-Kelly is standard in quantitative finance, used by Renaissance Technologies, Two Sigma, and other leading firms.

Optimal Fraction Proof

Maximize U(f) = E[ln(1+fR)] where R is return.

For full Kelly: f* maximizes U(f)

For constant relative risk aversion (CRRA) with coefficient γ:
U(f) = E[(1+fR)^(1-γ)]/(1-γ)

Half-Kelly corresponds to γ=2 (moderate risk aversion).

Power utility parameter γ = 2 generates optimal f = f*/2.

Common Half-Kelly Mistakes

Using half-Kelly without verifying strategy edge. Not reducing size after drawdowns. Ignoring correlation between strategies. Over-concentrating in single position. Failing to account for transaction costs. Using half-Kelly on non-Gaussian return distributions.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is half-Kelly suitable for beginners?

Yes, half-Kelly is a good starting point. Use quarter-Kelly (25%) initially until strategy proven. Graduate to half-Kelly after 200+ trades showing consistent edge.

How does half-Kelly handle losing streaks?

Half-Kelly reduces position size proportional to capital. After 25% drawdown, position sizes decrease by 25%, naturally reducing risk during difficult periods.

Can I use half-Kelly with leverage?

Yes, but reduce fraction accordingly. For 2x leverage, use quarter-Kelly (25%) to maintain equivalent risk level. Leverage amplifies both gains and losses.
